\chapter*{Introduction}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Introduction}

Evolutionary algorithms are a class of nature based optimisation algorithms predicated on the idea of "survival of the fitest" using the optimisation objective as "fitness", guiding the algorithm's search. However for complex objectives direct use of fitness might clog the algorithm in a false, local, optima, unable to reach true global optima. To solve this problem a variety of aproaches have been suggested such as archiving, where variety of individuals is kept by an archive. Or novelty search where fitness is replaced by "novelty", a difference in behaviour from rest of the population. Particularly novelty has been shown to work in constrained reinforcement learning tasks such as mazes. The notion of novelty for reinforcement learning is being even generalised outside of the evolutionary algorithms <citation here>. 
On the other hand there is a number of tasks where combination with fitness might enhance the novelty search, suggesting local competition like MAP-ELites or Dominated Search or other k-neighbours algortihm. These techniques however tend to be expansive for computation.
 
In this thesis we will characterise two reinforcement learning environments, Carpole and Lunar lander, suggest interpretable behavioral representation for them and apply different evolutionary algorithm techniques, such as differential evolution, itself being a mechanism to introduce controled diversity into the population. We will compare several lightweight applications of novelty combined with fitness, showing their viability and pitfalls on the tasks at hand and suggest best aproach for each of the environments.
In the chapter \ref{ch:agents} we will introduce terminology pertaining to agents we will be optimising. In chapter \ref{chap:environment} we will instroduce the aforementioned reinforcement learning tasks and detail the optimising algorithms in chapter \ref{chap:evolution}. In chapter \ref{ch:setup} we will review background of the experiments and finally in chapter \ref{ch:experiments} we will discuss the results of our experiments.
